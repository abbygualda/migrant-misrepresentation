Migrant
Misrepresentation

Newspapers play a critical role in articulating, shaping, and distributing public opinions. Media framing on migrants in the United States can powerfully influence how the public perceives the community. Understanding how newspapers discuss migrants can illuminate the rationale behind pro- or anti-immigration sentiments.


Despite the prominence of immigration-related debates, there is a lack of data-based analyses on how migrants are portrayed in American media.

About & Methodology

Starting from April 2, 2013, the AP (Associated Press) style books no longer permit the term “illegal immigrant” or the use of “illegal” to describe a person except for the use in the direct quotes. Following AP’s lead, major news outlets such as the Chicago Tribune, the Los Angeles Times, and USA Today abandoned the use of the phrase “illegal immigrant.”

Visualizing Data

Our research fills the gap by providing quantitative and qualitative examinations of around 4000 (n = 3,981) articles from 8 news agencies during 2010-2020. Three of our news agencies are national and other five are regional news, which represent the United States Northeast (New York Daily, Boston Globe), Midwest (The Capital Times, St. Louis Post-Dispatch), and West (Arizona Daily Star).
